Background

The postanalytical phase is a patient-safety process.

It begins when the laboratory decides whether a completed test result is appropriate for clinical use—not merely when the analyzer stops.

Every result reaching the electronic medical record has passed through checkpoints involving technical performance, specimen quality, plausibility, communication, and documentation. A numerically accurate measurement can still harm a patient if it is assigned to the wrong patient, reported with incorrect units, released without necessary context, or never communicated to the responsible clinician.

“The right result must reach the right clinician at the right time with the appropriate interpretation.”

Core educational content

Eight decisions behind a safe result.

01 / 08

Result verification

Verification asks whether a result is technically and clinically believable before it is released.

Was quality control acceptable?Did the analyzer perform correctly?Were instrument flags generated?Does it fit the patient’s history?Could specimen quality explain it?Is more testing needed before release?
Director’s question

Does this result make physiologic sense?

02 / 08

Delta checks

A delta check compares a current value with a previous result from the same patient. Large changes can reflect true deterioration—or wrong patient/specimen, contamination, dilution, interference, or analytical error.

Hemoglobin12.8 → 6.1
Potassium4.2 → 7.4
Creatinine0.8 → 4.3
Key distinction

A failed delta check does not mean “wrong.” It means “investigate before release.”

03 / 08

Autoverification

Autoverification releases results automatically only after predefined safety rules are satisfied. It can shorten turnaround time, standardize reporting, reduce transcription error, and preserve staff attention for exceptions.

  • QC acceptable
  • No disqualifying analyzer flags
  • Within analytical measurement range
  • Delta-check criteria satisfied
  • Acceptable hemolysis, icterus, and lipemia indices
  • No critical-result rule requiring manual action
Leadership role

Algorithms require validation, monitoring, and revision. Automation operationalizes judgment; it does not replace oversight.

04 / 08

Manual verification

Human review integrates laboratory data with the clinical record when rules cannot safely resolve an exception.

Review prior resultsInspect specimen and HIL indicesReview collection historyAssess analyzer flagsConfirm repeat testingSeek relevant clinical context
Resident pearl

Do not reflexively order a repeat. First ask what the technologist has already done and what additional information a repeat would provide.

05 / 08

Instrument flags

A flag is not a diagnosis. It is an alert requiring evaluation.

Platelet clumpsBlast flagCold agglutininsAspiration errorCarryover
  1. Determine why the flag occurred.
  2. Decide whether additional testing is needed.
  3. Determine whether manual review is indicated.
Never ignore an instrument flag.

Resolve or appropriately qualify it before release.

06 / 08

Interpretive comments

Useful comments explain limitations, known interferences, and specimen quality; they remain evidence-based and avoid directing medical management.

“The specimen demonstrated significant hemolysis. Internal validation supports reporting of this analyte; however, interpretation should consider potential interference. Repeat collection may be considered if the result is inconsistent with the clinical presentation.”
Quality test

Does the comment reduce ambiguity without overreaching?

07 / 08

Critical results

A critical result is associated with immediate patient risk and requires prompt communication under the laboratory’s approved policy. Examples may include critical potassium, glucose, calcium, platelet count, INR, or a positive blood culture; thresholds and workflows are institution-specific.

Closed-loop requirement

Verification, recipient identification, read-back, documentation, and escalation are parts of one process.

08 / 08

Common systems errors

Many postanalytical errors occur after an analyzer has produced an accurate result.

Incorrect patient reportWrong reference interval or unitsFailure to notifyDelayed reportingMisleading commentWrong recipientFailure to correct a report
Systems view

Ask which safeguard failed—and how the process can be redesigned to prevent recurrence.

Summary

Think like a laboratory director.

Before release, ask:

01

Verification is a clinical decision supported by laboratory data.

02

Delta checks are patient-safety prompts, not proof of error.

03

Autoverification improves efficiency but requires professional oversight.

04

Manual review integrates technical findings, specimen quality, and context.

05

Critical communication is incomplete until acknowledged and documented.

06

Many postanalytical errors are communication and systems failures.
