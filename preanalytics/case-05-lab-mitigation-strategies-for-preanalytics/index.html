Background

Mitigation begins by knowing where the failure entered the testing process

The laboratory testing as three connected phases: preanalytical, analytical, and post-analytical. Preanalytical work includes order entry, patient preparation, specimen collection, labeling, transport, and processing. Analytical work includes instrument function, reagent integrity, pipetting, assay performance, quality control, and result generation. Post-analytical work includes result review, reporting, documentation, and communication.

Residents should avoid treating these phases as silos. A preanalytical error may first appear as an analytical flag, and the safest response often requires post-analytical communication and quality review.

Core Educational Content

Laboratory mitigation converts specimen defects into controlled decisions

A

Analyzer and instrument safeguards

Real-time alarms may identify reagent depletion, mechanical failure, sample aspiration problems, clot detection, short sample, or results outside reportable limits.

B

Specimen-quality alerts

Hemolysis, icterus, lipemia, insufficient sample volume, clot flags, and tube acceptability checks can stop unreliable results before release.

C

Human review and intervention

Staff review flags, compare prior results, inspect specimens, call units, request recollection, and document the reason a result is suppressed or qualified.

D

Quality audits and root cause analysis

Alarm logs and rejection patterns can reveal recurring collection issues, training gaps, unit-specific problems, or process failures.

New Source Material

Performance monitoring extends mitigation into the analytical phase

The laboratory systems that keep instruments and assays trustworthy: equipment maintenance, calibration, quality control, proficiency testing, standard operating procedures, staff competency, and instrument-to-instrument comparisons.

QC1 QC2

Calibrator or maintenance changes should be visible in QC review before patient results are affected.

Equipment maintenance and calibration

Routine daily maintenance keeps the analyzer ready for use. Major maintenance, often every 6 months or yearly, requires verification before routine release of patient results.

Quality control testing

QC is generally run 2 to 3 times per day for each assay and after defined events such as calibration, reagent changes, troubleshooting, or maintenance.

Proficiency testing

Regulatory or accrediting groups distribute unknown material, commonly three times per year, so laboratories can compare performance with peer laboratories.

SOPs, training, and comparison studies

Standard operating procedures, training, competency assessment, and instrument-to-instrument comparisons convert individual checks into a durable quality system.

Mechanisms

Each alert should trigger a mechanism-based question

Hemolysis releases intracellular contents

In vitro hemolysis can increase or interfere with analytes such as potassium, LDH, AST, and some assays affected by spectrophotometric interference. The mitigation question is whether the result is analytically valid for that degree of hemolysis.

Contamination creates patterned abnormalities

EDTA carryover may produce high potassium with low calcium or magnesium. Heparin contamination can affect coagulation testing. IV fluid contamination can dilute multiple analytes in a clinically discordant pattern.

Short volume disrupts assay assumptions

Insufficient volume can cause aspiration errors or inadequate sample-to-additive ratios. Citrated plasma is especially sensitive because anticoagulant ratio is part of coagulation testing validity.

Identity uncertainty invalidates interpretation

Labeling problems are not fixed by an analytically precise result. If patient identity is uncertain, the result cannot safely support patient care.

Decision Lens

From flag to action

Select a flag to see the resident-level action focus.

Ask: which analytes are affected at this hemolysis index?

Apply assay-specific thresholds, suppress affected results when required, and document the specimen condition.

Ask: does this sample meet validated minimum volume and additive requirements?

Do not stretch an inadequate specimen into an unreliable result. Prioritize critical testing only under defined policy.

Ask: can patient identity be established under policy?

Identity defects usually require recollection. Document the event and feed it into safety review.

Summary

A practical resident framework

Detect

Use analyzer alarms, specimen indices, volume checks, label checks, and delta review as early warning systems.

Interpret

Connect the signal to a mechanism: hemolysis, contamination, short draw, clot, instrument failure, or identity defect.

Act

Suppress, qualify, recollect, escalate, or release based on validated policy and clinical urgency.

Improve

Document, audit, trend, educate, and update workflows so the same defect is less likely to recur.
