Background

Most laboratory errors begin before analysis, but they often reveal themselves on the CBC or smear

Approximately 60-70% of laboratory errors occur during the preanalytical phase. For CBCs and peripheral smears, these errors can be especially deceptive because they alter counts, distort cells, and change the apparent distribution of cells across the slide.

A resident reviewing a smear should start with specimen fitness: the correct tube, adequate fill, proper mixing, timely smear preparation, appropriate storage, acceptable smear thickness, correct staining, and examination in the zone of morphology.

Artifacts may mimic:

  • Acute leukemia or immature cell proliferation
  • True thrombocytopenia or thrombocytosis
  • Hemolytic anemia or renal-associated echinocytosis
  • False leukocytosis, leukopenia, or abnormal differential
  • Dysplastic leukocytes or toxic change

Core Educational Content

Preanalytical failure points that change CBCs and smears

Correct tube selection

CBC specimens should be collected in lavender-top K2EDTA or K3EDTA tubes to preserve cellular morphology and prevent clotting.

  • Serum tube: clot formation, platelet trapping, false thrombocytopenia.
  • Sodium citrate: dilutional decreases in hemoglobin, hematocrit, platelets, and WBC count unless correction is validated.
  • Heparin: blue background, poor leukocyte morphology, platelet clumping; rarely acceptable for manual smear review.

Underfilled EDTA tube

Modern experimental evidence supports falsely increased MCV and falsely decreased MCHC in severely underfilled spray-dried K2EDTA tubes. In Lippi et al. (2023), 3.0 mL K2EDTA tubes filled to 2.0 mL showed no clinically significant change, while 1.0, 0.5, and 0.2 mL fills produced increased MCV and decreased MCHC. The authors concluded that tubes filled to less than 67% of nominal volume should be rejected because the bias becomes clinically significant.

Fill volume MCV MCHC
2.0 mL (67%) No clinically significant change No significant change
1.0 mL (33%) Increased Decreased
0.5 mL (17%) Increased Decreased
0.2 mL (7%) Increased Decreased

A 2024 validation study reached a similar conclusion: a 3.0 mL tube containing only 0.5 mL blood showed MCV increased by approximately 2.4%, increased hematocrit, and decreased MCHC compared with a properly filled tube.

  • Historical teaching: excess EDTA was classically described as causing RBC water loss, shrinkage, and low MCV.
  • Current hypothesis: RBCs initially dehydrate in concentrated K2EDTA, then overswell after aspiration into isotonic analyzer diluent.
  • Modern spray-dried K2EDTA pattern: MCV falsely increased and MCHC falsely decreased.
  • WBCs: nuclear distortion and cytoplasmic vacuolization.
  • Platelets: swelling and degeneration.

As a laboratory director, know the EDTA tube type, additive formulation, analyzer platform, time from collection to analysis, and the laboratory's validated rejection threshold.

Inadequate mixing and clots

Failure to invert EDTA tubes 8-10 times can create microclots, platelet clumps, uneven cell distribution, and false thrombocytopenia.

If a clot is identified, the CBC should generally not be reported. Recollection is usually required.

Delayed smear preparation

Smears are best prepared within 2-3 hours. Delay causes neutrophil vacuoles and nuclear degeneration, lymphocyte degeneration, platelet swelling, and RBC echinocyte formation.

Storage temperature

Room temperature is ideal for routine smear preparation. Refrigeration can promote platelet clumping, echinocytes, and leukocyte degeneration; excessive heat can cause cell lysis and poor staining.

Smear geometry

  • Short smear: cells crowded.
  • Long smear: cells too sparse.
  • Wavy feather edge: uneven distribution.
  • Holes: interpretation difficult.

Wrong area examined

Differentials performed too close to the feather edge may falsely increase blasts, monocytes, reactive lymphocytes, and platelet clumps. Perform the differential in the zone of morphology.

EDTA-dependent platelet clumping

EDTA exposes cryptic platelet antigens; autoantibodies cause platelet aggregation. The analyzer reports very low platelets, while the smear shows large platelet clumps, often at the feather edge.

Never report a platelet count from a clumped specimen without smear correlation. Repeat in sodium citrate or ACD when appropriate.

Platelet satellitism

A rare EDTA phenomenon in which platelets adhere around neutrophils, producing false thrombocytopenia. Diagnosis requires smear review.

Cold agglutinins

Analyzer pattern: increased MCV, decreased RBC count, and increased MCHC, often above 37 g/dL. Smear shows large RBC aggregates. Warm the specimen to 37°C and repeat analysis.

Lipemia and hemolysis

Lipemia can falsely increase hemoglobin, MCH, and MCHC while the smear is generally normal. In vitro hemolysis usually has little CBC effect but may show ghost cells and debris.

Analyzer flags requiring smear correlation

  • Blast flag or immature granulocyte flag
  • Platelet clump flag or giant platelet flag
  • NRBC flag or atypical lymphocyte flag
  • RBC agglutination flag

CBC values that should trigger review

  • Platelets <100 or >700 x10^9/L
  • WBC >30 or <3 x10^9/L
  • New anemia or unexpected leukocytosis
  • Analyzer flags, delta check failure, or clinical concern

Platelet estimate

Estimate platelets in the zone of morphology, not the feather edge. Average 10 oil immersion fields and multiply by 15,000. Platelet clumps invalidate the estimate.

Mechanisms

Why these artifacts create convincing but misleading patterns

EDTA excess

Underfilling changes cell morphology

Severe underfilling of modern spray-dried K2EDTA tubes can falsely increase MCV and decrease MCHC. The likely mechanism is initial RBC dehydration in concentrated EDTA followed by overswelling after transfer into isotonic analyzer diluent.

EDTA antibodies

Pseudothrombocytopenia

EDTA-dependent antibodies aggregate platelets in vitro. The patient may have adequate circulating platelets, but the analyzer counts clumps poorly.

RBC agglutination

Cold agglutinins alter indices

Clumped RBCs are counted as fewer, larger particles, producing low RBC count, high MCV, and high MCHC until the sample is warmed and rerun.

Time and storage

Old cells look abnormal

Delayed smears can show vacuolated neutrophils, nuclear swelling, smudging, platelet swelling, and echinocytes that should not be overcalled as disease.

Slide physics

Distribution is not random

Large cells and platelet clumps concentrate near the feather edge. A differential from the wrong area can overrepresent abnormal-appearing cells.

Stain chemistry

pH shifts change color

High pH makes RBCs gray-blue; low pH makes RBCs orange. Understaining appears pale, while overstaining makes the slide excessively blue.
