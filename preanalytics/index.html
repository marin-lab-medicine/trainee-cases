Background

Interpretation begins before the specimen is collected.

A laboratory result is evidence. Its diagnostic meaning depends on what was plausible before the test was ordered.

History, examination, risk factors, disease prevalence, prior results, and clinical judgment establish a pretest probability. The test then modifies that probability. This is why the same numerical result can mean something very different in two patients.

Central principleA laboratory test does not diagnose disease. It changes the probability that disease is present.

Core educational content

Know what each performance measure actually answers.

Select a concept to examine its denominator, formula, and clinical use.

01 / 05

Analytical performance is not diagnostic performance

Analytical sensitivity

The smallest amount of analyte an assay can reliably detect. It asks: Can the assay detect the analyte?

Analytical specificity

The ability to measure the intended analyte while minimizing interference or cross-reactivity.

Clinical sensitivity

The proportion of patients with disease who test positive.

TP ÷ (TP + FN)

Clinical specificity

The proportion of patients without disease who test negative.

TN ÷ (TN + FP)

03 / 05

Predictive values describe this tested population

Positive predictive valuePPV = TP ÷ (TP + FP)

Given a positive result, what is the probability disease is present?

Negative predictive valueNPV = TN ÷ (TN + FN)

Given a negative result, what is the probability disease is absent?

Prevalence matters. As disease becomes rarer, false positives can outnumber true positives and PPV falls. NPV generally rises.

04 / 05

Likelihood ratios connect the test to Bayesian updating

Positive likelihood ratioLR+ = sensitivity ÷ (1 − specificity)

How much more likely is a positive result in disease than without disease?

Negative likelihood ratioLR− = (1 − sensitivity) ÷ specificity

How much less likely is a negative result in disease than without disease?

LR+ >10 often provides a large probability increaseLR− <0.1 often provides a large probability decrease

05 / 05

Common diagnostic interpretation errors

  • Ordering broad testing in patients with very low pretest probability
  • Ignoring disease prevalence or the population in which performance was validated
  • Confusing detection of a biomarker with diagnosis of a disease
  • Treating false-positive and false-negative results as impossibilities
  • Overriding the clinical context with a single laboratory result
  • Using a test when the result will not change management

Case 1 · Base-rate effect

A positive result in a healthy volunteer

A healthy 24-year-old is screened for a disease with 0.1% prevalence. The test has 99% sensitivity and 99% specificity. The result is positive.

What is the best interpretation?
Choose a response to see the diagnostic reasoning.
Expected per 100,000

100 have disease: about 99 test positive. Of 99,900 without disease: about 999 test positive. PPV ≈ 99 ÷ 1,098 = 9.0%.

Case 2 · Same number, different meaning

Two patients, identical troponin concentrations

Patient A has ischemic chest discomfort and dynamic ECG changes. Patient B has sepsis, renal dysfunction, and no ischemic symptoms. Both have the same elevated troponin concentration.

Which statement is most accurate?
Choose a response to see the diagnostic reasoning.
Laboratory consultation

Clarify the assay cutoff and serial pattern. Separate the measured biomarker, myocardial injury, and the clinical diagnosis of myocardial infarction.

Case 3 · Inappropriate testing

Low 4Ts score, positive HIT immunoassay

A hospitalized patient with a clear alternative cause of thrombocytopenia has a low 4Ts score. A PF4/heparin ELISA ordered without risk stratification is positive at low optical density.

What principle best explains the discordance?
Choose a response to see the diagnostic reasoning.
ASH-aligned stewardship

Use the 4Ts score first. Routine HIT laboratory testing is discouraged when the 4Ts score is low unless the score is uncertain because information is missing or inaccurate.

Case 4 · Unexpected discordance

Low PLASMIC score, ADAMTS13 activity <10%

A patient evaluated for thrombotic microangiopathy has a low PLASMIC score, but ADAMTS13 activity returns severely deficient.

What is the best laboratory response?
Choose a response to see the diagnostic reasoning.
Ask before excluding immune TTP

Was the specimen collected before plasma therapy? Were score inputs complete? Is the method affected by interference? Does inhibitor or anti-ADAMTS13 testing clarify the result? Does the evolving presentation change the pretest assessment?
