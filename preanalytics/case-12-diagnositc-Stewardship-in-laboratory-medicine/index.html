Background

More testing is not the same as better diagnosis.

Stewardship protects accuracy, timeliness, and patient outcomes across the entire testing process.

Working definition
Diagnostic stewardship is the coordinated use of testing to ensure that the right test is performed for the right patient at the right time, using the right specimen, and produces information that meaningfully changes care.

Not merely cost containment

Cost may improve when low-value testing falls, but it is not the primary endpoint. A stewardship decision may support an expensive or specialized assay when its result is sufficiently reliable, timely, and actionable.

Core educational content

Interrogate the test before it interrogates the patient.

Select a stewardship lens to review the decision a laboratory consultant should clarify.

01 / 05

What exactly are we trying to decide?

Translate “rule out everything” into a discriminating question. Define the phenotype, competing diagnoses, intended use, timing, and management options before selecting an assay.

Ask
Which result would change treatment, monitoring, disposition, or counseling?
Warning sign
No one can state what a positive, negative, or indeterminate result would change.
02 / 05

Probability determines what a result can mean.

Pretest probability integrates disease prevalence with patient-specific evidence. When probability is very low, even a sensitive test may generate more false-positive than true-positive results.

Bayesian update

Pretest odds × likelihood ratio = post-test odds. A test modifies probability; it does not replace clinical reasoning.

03 / 05

Use the simplest pathway that answers the question.

In-house

Prefer when a validated local assay provides the required performance and actionable turnaround. Use reflex rules and minimum repeat intervals where appropriate.

Specialized / send-out

Use for focused needs that require rare methods or expertise. Confirm first-line workup, specimen stability, transport, turnaround, interpretation, and result ownership.

04 / 05

Separate four questions that are often blurred together.

Analytical validity

Does the assay measure the target accurately and reproducibly?

Clinical validity

How strongly does the result associate with the condition or outcome?

Clinical utility

Does using the result improve a meaningful care decision or outcome?

Operational feasibility

Can the service deliver it safely, reliably, and sustainably?

05 / 05

Plan for every plausible result.

Consider false positives, false negatives, indeterminate findings, incidental findings, discordance, turnaround time, and who will communicate and act. A result without ownership is an unfinished test.

Consultant stance

Recommend, explain, document, and create a closed loop. Stewardship is an active clinical service, not an automated denial.

Case 1 · Pretest probability

A broad autoimmune panel in a low-risk patient

A 29-year-old has fatigue, diffuse pain, a normal examination, normal CBC and urinalysis, and no inflammatory features. A broad autoimmune panel is requested “to be thorough.”

What is the strongest stewardship response?

Resident consult

Identify organ-specific features, medication exposures, family history, and the working differential. Recommend focused first-line testing linked to the phenotype; explain that low pretest probability lowers positive predictive value.

Case 2 · Urgent specialized testing

Suspected TTP with a high PLASMIC score

A patient has thrombocytopenia, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, neurologic symptoms, and a high PLASMIC score. ADAMTS13 activity is available only as a send-out test.

Which plan best integrates testing with care?
Choose a response to reveal the diagnostic reasoning.
Preanalytic checkpoint

Whenever possible, obtain the ADAMTS13 specimen before plasma exchange or plasma infusion, which can affect measured activity. Document timing, treatment, transport, and the expected turnaround.

Case 3 · Emerging clinical utility

PET-positive lymphoma after therapy

A patient with treated diffuse large B-cell lymphoma has residual PET avidity. The team asks whether a commercial ctDNA MRD assay should determine salvage therapy.

Evaluation frame

Analytical: detection limit and tracking strategy. Clinical validity: association with relapse. Utility: evidence that acting on the result improves care. Operational: tissue access, turnaround, longitudinal sampling, reimbursement, and interpretation.

Case 4 · Premature advanced testing

Whole-exome sequencing before a basic hematologic evaluation

A patient with chronic anemia is referred for whole-exome sequencing. No smear review, reticulocyte count, iron studies, hemolysis evaluation, or family history is documented.

What should happen next?

Stepwise strategy

Confirm the CBC pattern and chronicity; review smear and reticulocytes; evaluate nutritional, hemolytic, renal, inflammatory, and acquired causes; build a pedigree; then select targeted or broader genetic testing with consent and a results plan.

A proposed assay with impressive precision

A vendor presents a precise assay with strong analytical validation. Clinicians are enthusiastic, but no study shows that the reported result changes management or outcomes in the intended population.

Summary

A reusable consultation rhythm.

Before approving, rejecting, or interpreting a test, work through these four moves.

01

Frame

Name the clinical question, probability, urgency, and decision threshold.

02

Fit

Match test, method, specimen, pathway, and turnaround to the intended use.

03

Forecast

Anticipate every result category, downstream action, and possible harm.

04

Follow through

Communicate, document, monitor utilization, and close the result loop.

Key take-home messages

  • Stewardship begins before the specimen is collected.
  • Pretest probability, analytical excellence, and thoughtful interpretation are inseparable.
  • Send-out testing is one component of diagnostic stewardship, not the goal itself.
  • Clinical utility matters more than analytical sophistication alone.
  • High-value laboratory medicine improves outcomes while reducing unnecessary testing and downstream harm.
