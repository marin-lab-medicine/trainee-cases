Preanalytical Errors: Inappropriate Tube Filling

Inappropriate filling is a collection error with potential analytic consequences. A tube may contain the correct patient specimen and the correct additive, yet still produce canceled, limited, or misleading results because the blood-to-additive ratio or specimen volume is wrong.

Teaching anchor: For citrate coagulation tubes, fill volume is part of the test system, not just a collection preference.

Proper fill

Designed blood-to-additive ratio and adequate plasma yield.

Underfill

Background

Preanalytical variables shape the result before analysis begins

Laboratory testing can be divided into preanalytical, analytical, and post-analytical phases. The preanalytical phase includes test ordering, patient preparation, specimen collection, labeling, transport, and processing. Many laboratory-related errors arise in this phase, before the analyzer ever receives a specimen.

Inappropriate filling belongs in the same family of collection problems as hemolysis, contamination, and labeling errors. Its danger is subtle: the tube may look plausible, but the specimen may no longer meet the assumptions built into the assay.

Core Educational Content

Tube type, additive, and fill volume are linked

Blue top

Sodium citrate plasma for coagulation testing. The intended blood-to-citrate ratio requires proper fill.

Red or gold top

Serum for drugs, serology, serum protein electrophoresis, and many chemistry tests.

Green top

Heparinized plasma for chemistry and selected specialized testing.

Purple top

EDTA anticoagulated blood for hematology and selected molecular or immunologic assays.

Plasma and serum are not interchangeable

Plasma is the liquid component of anticoagulated blood and contains clotting factors. Serum is produced after blood clots and clotting factors are consumed or removed with the clot. Test methods are validated for specific specimen types, so collection tube choice matters.

Key concept

Plasma = serum + clotting factors. Serum = plasma - clotting factors.

Correct tube, wrong volume

Underfilling can alter the blood to additive ratio and reduce the number of available tests that can be performed.

Correct fill, unusual hematocrit

Very high hematocrit reduces plasma volume, reducing the number of tests that may be performed.

Correct specimen, too little material

Even when chemistry is valid, low plasma or serum yield may limit the number of tests that can be performed.

Mechanisms

Why inappropriate filling changes laboratory interpretation

Excess citrate relative to blood

Sodium citrate anticoagulates by binding calcium. If a citrate tube is underfilled, the fixed citrate volume is too high for the amount of blood collected. During testing, this may leave insufficient free calcium after recalcification and falsely prolong clotting times.

Reduced specimen volume

An underfilled tube yields less plasma or serum after processing. The specimen may support only a limited number of assays, force test prioritization, or require recollection.

Hematocrit-dependent plasma fraction

A properly filled tube can still be problematic when hematocrit is markedly elevated. Less plasma is present for the same citrate volume, so the citrate-to-plasma ratio is effectively increased.

Summary

Key takeaways for resident practice

Fill volume is analytic context

A specimen is not acceptable simply because the correct tube was used. Fill volume can determine whether the assay assumptions remain valid.

Citrate tubes are high-yield

Underfilled blue-top tubes can produce excess citrate relative to blood and may falsely prolong clotting assays.

Volume limits matter

Low plasma or serum yield can force cancellations, test prioritization, or recollection even when the chemistry of the specimen is otherwise acceptable.

Hematocrit can change the ratio

Markedly elevated hematocrit can create a citrate-to-plasma mismatch despite a tube reaching the fill mark.
