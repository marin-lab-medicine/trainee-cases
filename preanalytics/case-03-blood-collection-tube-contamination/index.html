Collection Tubes and Order of Draw

Tube additives explain many contamination patterns

Collection tubes are designed for specific specimen types and test families. Citrate tubes support coagulation testing, serum tubes are used for many chemistry, drug, serology, and protein studies, and anticoagulated plasma tubes support selected chemistry or specialized assays.

The order of draw matters because additives can carry into subsequent tubes through backflow, needle or syringe contamination, improper transfer, or mislabeling. Residents should interpret implausible results in the context of the tube sequence and specimen processing history.

Specimen vocabulary

Plasma contains clotting factors. Serum is plasma after clotting factors are removed during clot formation.

Blue top

Citrated plasma for coagulation studies. Underfilling or contamination can alter clotting times.

Red or gold top

Serum for drugs, serology, serum protein electrophoresis, and other chemistry assays. Carry over can shorten clotting times, and cause micro-clots.

Green top

Heparinized plasma for chemistry testing. Carryover can prolong coagulation tests.

Purple top

EDTA specimens for hematology and selected specialized tests. Carryover can raise potassium and chelate divalent ions.

Contamination Mechanisms

Common routes and laboratory signatures

Incorrect order of draw

A non-heparin or chemistry tube collected after an additive tube may receive residual additive through backflow or shared collection equipment.

Improper transfer

Pouring blood between tubes or transferring from a contaminated syringe can introduce heparin or EDTA into the wrong specimen.

Line contamination

Drawing from an IV catheter or central line without adequate flushing and discard can dilute blood with IV fluid or heparin flush.

Labeling or handling error

A tube may be mislabeled, handled as the wrong specimen type, or exposed to equipment that previously contacted an additive.

Incomplete clotting

Recent heparin exposure or inadequate clotting time can leave residual fibrinogen in serum and create confusing protein electrophoresis patterns.
