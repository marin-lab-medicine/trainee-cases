Background

Every test is built here or purchased elsewhere.

The decision is clinical and operational, not a preference for a particular vendor.

A send-out test provides access to expertise or capacity that the health-system laboratory does not maintain internally.

External testing is often appropriate for low-volume, technically specialized, proprietary, rare-disease, or resource-constrained assays. The tradeoff is less direct control and a longer chain of custody.

Core teaching conceptThe best test is the one that provides sufficient clinical value, analytical performance, and actionable turnaround time through a feasible pathway.
In-house

Build internally

  • CBC, CMP, troponin, PT/INR
  • Rapid turnaround
  • Direct analytical oversight
  • Requires validation, staff, space, and sustainable volume
Send-out

Purchase externally

  • Rare genetics and specialized autoimmune testing
  • Access to focused expertise
  • Longer turnaround and transport dependencies
  • Requires utilization and vendor oversight

Core educational content

Consultation starts by classifying the question.

Select each lens to see what the resident should clarify and where the request belongs.

01 / 05

Will the result answer the clinical question?

Define the phenotype, differential diagnosis, intended use, and decision the result will change. Look for an equivalent approved test before seeking a new vendor.

Resident contribution
Test selection, clinical context, interpretive limitations
Escalation trigger
Discordant indication, duplication, or uncertain clinical utility
02 / 05

Equivalent names do not guarantee equivalent evidence.

Compare analyte, methodology, specimen type, reportable range, reference interval, decision thresholds, interfering substances, and intended-use population.

Example

An immunoassay and a mass spectrometry assay may target the same analyte but differ in cross-reactivity, sensitivity, specificity, and result comparability.

03 / 05

Feasibility must be settled before collection.

Confirm source, container, minimum and preferred volume, processing, stability, temperature, timing, and shipping-day restrictions in the current performing-laboratory catalog.

Do not improvise.

Requirements can change. Confirm the active test directory and consult send-out staff before an unusual or irreplaceable specimen is collected.

04 / 05

Route the question to the team that owns the work.

Send-out staff / customer service

How to order, requisition status, kit delivery, pickup, vendor website, accession, and routine transport.

Resident / laboratory physician

Right test for this patient, method differences, test limitations, unusual interpretation, or a staff-referred scientific question.

05 / 05

New pathways need deliberate review.

A request for a new test or reference laboratory should follow the institution's intake and approval process. Review clinical need, alternatives, performance, logistics, interface, billing, volume, and result ownership before go-live.

Local policy note

The source document directs new requests to the Laboratory Advancement and Best Practice Committee and Dr. Jenkins. Confirm the current committee name, intake form, and contact locally.

Summary

Diagnostic excellence includes operational excellence.

Before collection

Choose and prepare

  • Define the clinical question
  • Check approved equivalents
  • Compare methods when relevant
  • Confirm current specimen requirements
During processing

Protect and communicate

  • Verify patient, order, and integrity
  • Preserve stability and temperature
  • Meet packaging and cutoff requirements
  • Escalate constraints before viability is lost
After testing

Interpret and close the loop

  • Recognize method-dependent results
  • Verify interface and report routing
  • Communicate critical findings
  • Document exceptions and improve the process

Five messages to carry forward

  1. Send-out testing is a diagnostic resource, not merely a transaction.
  2. The newest or most expensive test is not automatically the best test.
  3. Stewardship begins before the specimen is collected.
  4. Every handoff can preserve or erode diagnostic value.
  5. Residents add value through clinical reasoning, method knowledge, and thoughtful escalation.
